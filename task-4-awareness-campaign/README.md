# Task 4 — Cybersecurity Awareness Campaign

## What This Task Was About

The first three tasks were all about responding to an incident after it happened. This task flipped it — I had to think about how to stop it from happening in the first place. That meant writing a proper email security policy for STC and designing a full employee awareness campaign around it.

---

## What I Produced

### Part 1 — Email Security Policy

I wrote a formal email security policy intended for all STC employees. The whole point of it was to give people clear, simple rules so they don't fall for something like the `Payslip.xlsm` phishing attack we investigated in Task 2.

**Who it applies to:** All STC employees and subsidiaries

**Who maintains it:** Information Security team — they're responsible for keeping it updated

**The rules I focused on:**

Sending emails:
- Double check who you're sending to before hitting send, especially if autocomplete fills in the address — it's easy to accidentally send sensitive information to the wrong person

Opening attachments and links:
- If you weren't expecting an attachment, don't open it — even if it looks like it's from someone you know
- Don't open attachments from senders you don't recognise
- Don't click links in emails unless you're confident the link is legitimate

---

### Part 2 — 28-Day Awareness Campaign

I designed a 4-phase campaign running over 28 days to actually get employees engaged with cybersecurity awareness — not just send them a document they'll ignore.

**Campaign period:** April – May 2026  
**Audience:** All STC employees  
**Main goal:** Make sure employees can recognise phishing, know the basics of cybersecurity, and know exactly how to report a suspicious email

**How the campaign is structured:**

**Week 1 — Infographics (Days 1–7)**
Simple visual content covering the key topics: how to spot phishing emails, password security, mobile device safety, and email dos and don'ts. The goal is to make the content easy to absorb without overwhelming people.

**Weeks 2–3 — Competitions (Days 8–21)**
Two rounds to get people actually participating:
- Round 1: Simulated phishing emails sent to all employees — whoever reports it first using the phishing report button wins a prize. This teaches people to act on what they learned, not just read about it.
- Round 2: A cipher posted on screens inside STC offices — first to decode it and submit wins. This re-engages anyone who missed round 1 and makes security feel less like a lecture.

**Week 4 — Training and Assessment (Days 22–28)**
A formal training course with quizzes on everything covered in the campaign. This is where the knowledge gets tested and reinforced.

## What I Learned

The technical tasks taught me how to investigate and respond to incidents. This task taught me that most incidents could be avoided entirely if employees just knew what to look out for. Writing the policy made me realise how many small mistakes people make with email without realising the risk — like trusting autocomplete or opening attachments from familiar-looking names. And designing the campaign taught me that gamification actually works — people engage with competitions and simulations in a way they never would with a standard training document.
